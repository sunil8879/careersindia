Definition & Concept

Concept: A phrasal verb is a multi-word verb made up of a main verb and at least one particle (a preposition or an adverb). The meaning of the phrasal verb is often idiomatic, meaning it is very different from the meanings of the individual words.

Why are they important?

Phrasal verbs are extremely common in everyday, informal English. Mastering them is essential for understanding native speakers and for success in Reading Comprehension, Cloze Tests, and Sentence Completion questions in competitive exams.

  • `give` (to offer) + `up` (direction) → give up (to quit)
  • `look` (to see) + `after` (position) → look after (to take care of)
  • `break` (to shatter) + `down` (direction) → break down (to stop functioning)

Rules & Syntax

Key Syntactical Categories

  • Transitive vs. Intransitive:
    • Transitive phrasal verbs need a direct object. (e.g., *She turned down the offer.*)
    • Intransitive phrasal verbs do not take an object. (e.g., *The car broke down.*)
  • Separable vs. Inseparable:
    • Separable (Transitive): The object can come between the verb and the particle. (e.g., *She turned the offer down.* OR *She turned down the offer.*)
      Note: If the object is a pronoun, it MUST come in between. (e.g., *She turned it down.*)
    • Inseparable (Transitive or Intransitive): The verb and particle must stay together. (e.g., *I will look into the matter.* NOT *I will look the matter into.*)

How to Identify Phrasal Verbs

Identifying phrasal verbs is primarily about vocabulary and context, not a specific grammatical trick.

A 3-Step Guide to Identification

  1. 1. Look for a `Verb + Particle` combination: Spot a main verb followed immediately by a small word like `up`, `down`, `in`, `on`, `off`, `away`, `out`, `about`.
  2. 2. Check the Meaning: Ask yourself if the combination has a figurative or idiomatic meaning that is different from the individual words. For example, in "He looked up the word," 'look up' doesn't mean to physically look in an upward direction; it means 'to research'.
  3. 3. Differentiate from Prepositional Verbs: In a simple prepositional verb, the verb and preposition keep their literal meanings (e.g., "He walked up the hill."). In a phrasal verb, the meaning is new and idiomatic (e.g., "He made up a story.").

100 Essential Phrasal Verbs

  1. Account for: to explain or give a reason for.
    Example: The bad weather accounts for the delay.
  2. Act on: to take action because of information received.
    Example: The police decided to act on the anonymous tip.
  3. Add up: to make sense or seem reasonable.
    Example: His story just doesn't add up.
  4. Ask for: to request something.
    Example: She asked for a promotion at work.
  5. Back down: to withdraw your claim or demand.
    Example: He refused to back down during the argument.
  6. Back up: to support or to make a copy of data.
    Example: Please back up all your important files.
  7. Blow up: to explode; to inflate.
    Example: The rebels tried to blow up the bridge.
  8. Break down: to stop working (machine); to lose emotional control.
    Example: The car broke down on the way.
  9. Break in/into: to enter a building by force.
    Example: Someone broke into our house last night.
  10. Break up: to end a relationship.
    Example: After many arguments, they decided to break up.
  11. Bring about: to cause something to happen.
    Example: The new CEO brought about many positive changes.
  12. Bring up: to raise a child; to mention a topic.
    Example: Don't bring up that sensitive topic again.
  13. Brush up on: to improve your knowledge or skill in something.
    Example: I need to brush up on my Spanish before my trip to Madrid.
  14. Burn out: to become extremely tired from overworking.
    Example: If you work 12 hours a day, you will eventually burn out.
  15. Call for: to require or demand.
    Example: This situation calls for immediate action.
  16. Call off: to cancel.
    Example: They had to call off the wedding.
  17. Calm down: to become less angry or upset.
    Example: Please calm down and tell me what happened.
  18. Carry on: to continue.
    Example: Please carry on with your work while I am away.
  19. Carry out: to perform a task or an order.
    Example: The soldiers carried out the captain's orders.
  20. Catch up: to reach the same level as someone else.
    Example: She was ill, so she has to work hard to catch up.
  21. Check in: to register at a hotel or airport.
    Example: We need to check in at least two hours before the flight.
  22. Check out: to leave a hotel; to investigate.
    Example: We must check out of the hotel by noon. Let's check out that new restaurant.
  23. Cheer up: to become happier.
    Example: I bought her flowers to cheer her up.
  24. Come across: to find by chance.
    Example: I came across some old letters in the attic.
  25. Come up with: to think of an idea or plan.
    Example: He came up with a great solution to the problem.
  26. Count on: to rely or depend on someone.
    Example: You can always count on me for help.
  27. Cut down on: to reduce the consumption of something.
    Example: I'm trying to cut down on caffeine.
  28. Do away with: to abolish; to get rid of.
    Example: The company decided to do away with the old policy.
  29. Do over: to do something again.
    Example: The teacher made him do the assignment over.
  30. Drop by/in: to visit informally for a short time.
    Example: Please drop by for a cup of coffee sometime.
  31. Drop out: to quit a course, school, or competition.
    Example: He dropped out of college after the first year.
  32. Fall apart: to break into pieces; to fail.
    Example: The old chair is falling apart.
  33. Fall behind: to fail to keep up with others.
    Example: If you miss too many classes, you will fall behind.
  34. Figure out: to understand or solve something.
    Example: I can't figure out why the computer isn't working.
  35. Fill out: to complete a form.
    Example: Please fill out this application form.
  36. Find out: to discover information.
    Example: We need to find out when the event starts.
  37. Get along with: to have a good relationship with someone.
    Example: Do you get along with your new boss?
  38. Get away with: to escape punishment for a wrongdoing.
    Example: He cheated on the test and got away with it.
  39. Get back at: to take revenge on someone.
    Example: She wanted to get back at him for his mean comments.
  40. Get over: to recover from an illness or disappointment.
    Example: It took him months to get over the loss of his pet.
  41. Get rid of: to eliminate something.
    Example: We need to get rid of this old furniture.
  42. Give in: to surrender.
    Example: The army refused to give in despite being outnumbered.
  43. Give up: to stop trying; to quit a habit.
    Example: He decided to give up smoking.
  44. Go after: to pursue someone or something.
    Example: The police officer went after the thief.
  45. Go on: to continue; to happen.
    Example: Please go on with your story.
  46. Go through: to experience something difficult.
    Example: She has gone through a lot of hardship.
  47. Grow up: to become an adult.
    Example: What do you want to be when you grow up?
  48. Hand in: to submit.
    Example: All students must hand in their reports by Monday.
  49. Hand out: to distribute.
    Example: The teacher will hand out the exam papers.
  50. Hang out: to spend time relaxing informally.
    Example: We used to hang out at the mall after school.
  51. Hang up: to end a phone call.
    Example: Don't hang up, I haven't finished talking!
  52. Hold on: to wait.
    Example: Please hold on for a moment.
  53. Keep on: to continue doing something.
    Example: If you keep on practicing, you will improve.
  54. Let down: to disappoint.
    Example: He felt that his team had let him down.
  55. Look after: to take care of.
    Example: Can you look after my dog this weekend?
  56. Look forward to: to anticipate with pleasure.
    Example: I look forward to meeting you in person.
  57. Look into: to investigate.
    Example: The committee will look into the allegations.
  58. Look up: to find information in a reference book.
    Example: I had to look up the word in the dictionary.
  59. Look up to: to admire and respect someone.
    Example: He has always looked up to his older brother.
  60. Make up: to invent a story; to reconcile.
    Example: Is that true, or did you make it up?
  61. Make up for: to compensate for something.
    Example: He tried to make up for his mistake by buying her a gift.
  62. Pass away: to die (polite term).
    Example: The former president passed away this morning.
  63. Pass out: to faint.
    Example: She passed out from the heat.
  64. Pay back: to return money owed.
    Example: I will pay you back next week.
  65. Pick up: to collect someone or something.
    Example: Can you pick up some milk on your way home?
  66. Point out: to draw attention to something.
    Example: She pointed out several errors in the report.
  67. Put off: to postpone.
    Example: The meeting was put off until next Friday.
  68. Put on: to wear clothes; to gain weight.
    Example: Put on your jacket, it's cold outside.
  69. Put up with: to tolerate.
    Example: I cannot put up with his constant complaining.
  70. Run into: to meet unexpectedly.
    Example: I ran into my old teacher at the mall.
  71. Run out of: to have none left.
    Example: We have run out of sugar.
  72. Set up: to establish; to arrange.
    Example: He wants to set up his own business.
  73. Show off: to boast or try to impress people.
    Example: He's always showing off his new sports car.
  74. Show up: to arrive.
    Example: We waited for an hour, but he never showed up.
  75. Shut up: to stop talking (impolite).
    Example: The teacher told the noisy students to shut up.
  76. Stand for: to represent or to tolerate.
    Example: 'USA' stands for the United States of America. I will not stand for such behavior.
  77. Take after: to resemble a family member.
    Example: She really takes after her mother.
  78. Take off: to leave the ground (plane); to remove clothes.
    Example: Please take off your shoes before entering.
  79. Take over: to take control.
    Example: The new CEO will take over in January.
  80. Talk into: to persuade someone to do something.
    Example: He talked me into going to the party.
  81. Think over: to consider something carefully.
    Example: I need some time to think it over.
  82. Throw away: to discard something.
    Example: Don't throw away those old newspapers.
  83. Try on: to put on clothes to see if they fit.
    Example: You should try on the shirt before you buy it.
  84. Turn down: to reject an offer; to reduce volume.
    Example: He turned down the job offer. Can you turn down the music?
  85. Turn into: to transform into something else.
    Example: The caterpillar will turn into a butterfly.
  86. Turn off: to deactivate a machine or light.
    Example: Please turn off the lights when you leave.
  87. Turn on: to activate a machine or light.
    Example: Can you turn on the television?
  88. Turn up: to arrive unexpectedly; to increase volume.
    Example: He finally turned up an hour late.
  89. Use up: to use all of a supply of something.
    Example: We used up all the milk.
  90. Wake up: to stop sleeping.
    Example: I wake up at 6 AM every day.
  91. Watch out: to be careful.
    Example: Watch out! There's a car coming.
  92. Wear off: to fade away or disappear gradually.
    Example: The effect of the painkiller will soon wear off.
  93. Work out: to exercise; to find a solution.
    Example: We need to work out a compromise.
