ADVANCED GRAMMAR: TAGS, PARALLELISM & INVERSION

Question Tags

Concept: A question tag is a short question added to the end of a statement for confirmation.
Core Rule: Positive Statement + Negative Tag. Negative Statement + Positive Tag.

50 Examples:

  • He is a doctor, isn't he?
    Positive statement with 'is' takes negative tag 'isn't'.
  • She isn't coming, is she?
    Negative statement with 'isn't' takes positive tag 'is'.
  • They play cricket, don't they?
    Simple present verb takes 'do/don't' in the tag.
  • He likes coffee, doesn't he?
    Singular present verb takes 'does/doesn't' in the tag.
  • You don't know the answer, do you?
    Negative 'don't know' takes positive 'do'.
  • She went to the market, didn't she?
    Simple past verb takes 'did/didn't' in the tag.
  • They didn't complete the task, did they?
    Negative 'didn't complete' takes positive 'did'.
  • We are studying, aren't we?
    Helping verb 'are' is used in the tag.
  • He was sleeping, wasn't he?
    Helping verb 'was' is used in the tag.
  • They weren't playing, were they?
    Negative 'weren't' takes positive 'were'.
  • She has finished her work, hasn't she?
    Helping verb 'has' is used in the tag.
  • They have arrived, haven't they?
    Helping verb 'have' is used in the tag.
  • He hadn't left, had he?
    Negative 'hadn't' takes positive 'had'.
  • You had seen it before, hadn't you?
    Helping verb 'had' is used in the tag.
  • He can swim, can't he?
    Modal 'can' is used in the tag.
  • She will come tomorrow, won't she?
    The negative tag for 'will' is 'won't'.
  • We should go now, shouldn't we?
    Modal 'should' is used in the tag.
  • They must follow the rules, mustn't they?
    Modal 'must' is used in the tag.
  • You couldn't do it, could you?
    Negative 'couldn't' takes positive 'could'.
  • It wouldn't be a problem, would it?
    Negative 'wouldn't' takes positive 'would'.
  • I am late, aren't I?
    Special Case: The tag for 'I am' is 'aren't I?'.
  • I'm not bothering you, am I?
    Special Case: The tag for 'I am not' is the standard 'am I?'.
  • Let's start the meeting, shall we?
    Special Case: Suggestions with 'Let's' always take the tag 'shall we?'.
  • Close the door, will you?
    Special Case: Imperatives (commands/requests) take the tag 'will you?' or 'won't you?'.
  • Please help me, won't you?
    Special Case: Polite requests often use 'won't you?'.
  • Everybody has come, haven't they?
    Special Case: Indefinite pronouns like 'everybody' are singular in the statement ('has') but take a plural pronoun ('they') in the tag.
  • Someone is at the door, aren't they?
    Special Case: 'Someone' is singular ('is') but takes the plural 'they' in the tag. The verb 'are' matches 'they'.
  • Nobody called, did they?
    Special Case: 'Nobody' is negative, so it takes a positive tag. It also uses the plural pronoun 'they'.
  • Nothing was lost, was it?
    Special Case: 'Nothing' is negative and refers to a thing, so it takes a positive tag with the pronoun 'it'.
  • He hardly ever speaks, does he?
    Special Case: Negative adverbs like 'hardly', 'seldom', 'rarely' make the statement negative, requiring a positive tag.
  • She seldom visits, does she?
    Special Case: 'seldom' is negative, so the tag is positive.
  • This is your car, isn't it?
    Special Case: The subject 'This' takes the pronoun 'it' in the tag.
  • Those are my books, aren't they?
    Special Case: The subject 'Those' takes the pronoun 'they' in the tag.
  • There is a problem, isn't there?
    Special Case: Sentences starting with 'There' use 'there' as the subject in the tag.
  • There were many people, weren't there?
    Special Case: The verb and subject 'there' are repeated from the statement.
  • He has a car, doesn't he?
    Special Case: When 'has' is the main verb (meaning possession), the tag uses 'do/does'.
  • They have lunch at 1 PM, don't they?
    Special Case: When 'have' is a main verb, use 'do/don't'.
  • He used to live here, didn't he?
    Special Case: For 'used to', the tag is 'didn't'.
  • You ought to go, shouldn't you?
    Special Case: The tag for 'ought to' is usually 'shouldn't'.
  • One must do one's duty, mustn't one?
    The subject 'one' is repeated in the tag.
  • The jury has given its verdict, hasn't it?
    A collective noun acting as a unit is singular ('it').
  • The team are fighting, aren't they?
    A collective noun acting as individuals is plural ('they').
  • A few people were present, weren't they?
    'A few' is positive, so it takes a negative tag.
  • Few people were present, were they?
    'Few' is negative, so it takes a positive tag.
  • A little sugar was added, wasn't it?
    'A little' is positive.
  • Little progress has been made, has it?
    'Little' is negative.
  • The sun is a star, isn't it?
    Standard positive statement with 'is'.
  • Dogs can't fly, can they?
    Standard negative statement with 'can't'.
  • She reads a lot, doesn't she?
    Simple present singular verb.
  • You wrote this, didn't you?
    Simple past verb.

Parallelism (Parallel Structure)

Concept: Parallelism is the principle that items in a list or comparison must have the same grammatical form (e.g., noun with noun, -ing with -ing, clause with clause). This creates balance and clarity.

50 Examples (25 Incorrect vs. Correct Pairs):

  • Incorrect: She likes swimming, hiking, and to ride a bicycle.
    Error: Mixes gerunds (-ing forms) with an infinitive (to + verb).
  • Correct: She likes swimming, hiking, and riding a bicycle.
    Correction: All items in the list are now parallel gerunds.
  • Incorrect: The report was written clearly, concisely, and it was accurate.
    Error: Mixes adverbs with a clause.
  • Correct: The report was written clearly, concisely, and accurately.
    Correction: All three items are now adverbs modifying the verb 'written'.
  • Incorrect: He was asked to stop talking, to put away his phone, and that he should pay attention.
    Error: Mixes infinitives with a noun clause.
  • Correct: He was asked to stop talking, to put away his phone, and to pay attention.
    Correction: The structure is now a parallel list of three infinitive phrases.
  • Incorrect: The manager is not only a good leader but also he is a great mentor.
    Error: Correlative conjunctions join a noun phrase (`a good leader`) with a clause.
  • Correct: The manager is not only a good leader but also a great mentor.
    Correction: Both items are now parallel noun phrases.
  • Incorrect: To succeed is opening a new opportunity.
    Error: Compares an infinitive (`To succeed`) with a gerund (`opening`).
  • Correct: To succeed is to open a new opportunity.
    Correction: Both sides of the linking verb 'is' are now parallel infinitives.
  • Incorrect: The witness spoke calmly and with confidence.
    Error: Mixes an adverb (`calmly`) with a prepositional phrase.
  • Correct: The witness spoke calmly and confidently.
    Correction: Both items are now parallel adverbs.
  • Incorrect: My friend is kind, generous, and likes to help.
    Error: Mixes adjectives (`kind`, `generous`) with a verb phrase.
  • Correct: My friend is kind, generous, and helpful.
    Correction: All items in the list are now parallel adjectives.
  • Incorrect: He is a man of great intelligence and who has a lot of courage.
    Error: Mixes a prepositional phrase with a relative clause.
  • Correct: He is a man of great intelligence and of great courage.
    Correction: Both items are now parallel prepositional phrases.
  • Incorrect: The company's objectives are to increase profits, reducing costs, and to improve quality.
    Error: Mixes infinitives with a gerund.
  • Correct: The company's objectives are to increase profits, to reduce costs, and to improve quality.
    Correction: All items are now parallel infinitives.
  • Incorrect: What you say is less important than the way you are acting.
    Error: Compares a noun clause (`What you say`) with a noun phrase.
  • Correct: What you say is less important than what you do.
    Correction: Both items are now parallel noun clauses.
  • Incorrect: The teacher told the students that they should study hard and to complete their assignments.
  • Correct: The teacher told the students that they should study hard and complete their assignments.
  • Incorrect: His new car is sleek, fast, and has a lot of power.
  • Correct: His new car is sleek, fast, and powerful.
  • Incorrect: The presentation was both informative and it was also entertaining.
  • Correct: The presentation was both informative and entertaining.
  • Incorrect: They decided to paint the house, repairing the roof, and to fix the fence.
  • Correct: They decided to paint the house, repair the roof, and fix the fence.
  • Incorrect: The candidate's experience is better than his opponent.
  • Correct: The candidate's experience is better than his opponent's. (or `that of his opponent`)
  • Incorrect: She is a writer, a mother, and works as a teacher.
  • Correct: She is a writer, a mother, and a teacher.
  • Incorrect: I would rather work at home than going to the office.
  • Correct: I would rather work at home than go to the office.
  • Incorrect: He is either a fool or is a madman.
  • Correct: He is either a fool or a madman.
  • Incorrect: The city is famous for its museums, its parks, and you can visit the old castle.
  • Correct: The city is famous for its museums, its parks, and its old castle.
  • Incorrect: The benefits of exercise include strengthening the heart, to lose weight, and improving your mood.
  • Correct: The benefits of exercise include strengthening the heart, losing weight, and improving your mood.
  • Incorrect: He is a politician with a strong will and who is very determined.
  • Correct: He is a politician with a strong will and great determination.
  • Incorrect: The manager praised the team for their hard work and because they were dedicated.
  • Correct: The manager praised the team for their hard work and their dedication.
  • Incorrect: It is easier to tell the truth than living with a lie.
  • Correct: It is easier to tell the truth than to live with a lie.
  • Incorrect: The storm was powerful, destructive, and lasted for hours.
  • Correct: The storm was powerful, destructive, and long-lasting.
  • Incorrect: The instructions were to assemble the parts, connecting the wires, and to test the device.
  • Correct: The instructions were to assemble the parts, connect the wires, and test the device.

Inversion

Concept: Inversion is the reversal of the normal subject-verb word order. The verb (or helping verb) comes before the subject.

Where and How It Is Used

  • **In Questions:** (e.g., `Are you coming?`)
  • **After Negative/Limiting Adverbs:** When a sentence begins with an expression like `No sooner`, `Hardly`, `Never`, or `Not only`, the helping verb and subject must be inverted for formal emphasis.
  • **In Conditional Sentences:** To form formal conditional clauses without 'if'.

50 Examples:

  • Are you ready?
  • Did he finish the report?
  • Have they arrived yet?
  • Will she accept the offer?
  • Where is the nearest station?
  • Hardly had I left the house when it started to rain.
  • No sooner did he see the police than he ran away.
  • Not only does he play the guitar, but he also sings.
  • Never have I seen such a beautiful sight.
  • Seldom do we get a chance to see him.
  • Scarcely had the movie started when the power went out.
  • Under no circumstances should you leave this building.
  • At no time were the students allowed to use their phones.
  • In no way can he be held responsible for the accident.
  • Little did I know about the surprise they had planned.
  • Not until I saw it with my own eyes did I believe it.
  • Rarely do we see such talent.
  • Hardly ever does she complain.
  • No sooner had the teacher entered the class than the students became silent.
  • Never again will I trust him.
  • Not a single word did she say.
  • Not only is he intelligent, but he is also hardworking.
  • Under no circumstances are you to enter this room.
  • Only after finishing his homework was he allowed to play.
  • Only then did I understand the problem.
  • Only in this way can you succeed.
  • Scarcely had I finished reading when she called.
  • Seldom has a politician been so honest.
  • Rarely does it snow in this part of the country.
  • Little did she realize the danger she was in.
  • Had I known the truth, I would have acted differently. (Instead of 'If I had known...')
  • Were I you, I would not accept the offer. (Instead of 'If I were you...')
  • Should you need any help, do not hesitate to ask. (Instead of 'If you should need...')
  • Had he studied harder, he would have passed.
  • Were the government to announce the policy, there would be protests.
  • Should the train be late, we will miss our flight.
  • Had I been there, this would not have happened.
  • Were she to apologize, I would forgive her.
  • Should you change your mind, please let me know.
  • So beautiful was the painting that I couldn't take my eyes off it.
  • So fast did he run that no one could catch him.
  • Such was the force of the storm that trees were uprooted.
  • On the hill stands a large castle.
  • Around the corner came the bus.
  • Down the street marched the soldiers.
  • So great was his courage that he was awarded a medal.
  • So confusing were the instructions that nobody could understand them.
  • Along the river ran the children.
  • Into the room walked the mysterious stranger.
