Number Series

Core Concept: Analyzing the Gap

Question

What is the first and most important step to solve any number series problem?

Answer

Analyze the "gap" or difference between the consecutive numbers. The nature of this gap tells you which type of operation to look for.

Explanation

The speaker outlines a clear strategy based on the size of the gap:

  • Slowly Increasing Gap (Small Difference): The pattern is likely based on Addition. Check the difference between the numbers.
  • Rapidly Increasing Gap (Large Difference): The pattern is likely based on Multiplication, Squares, or Cubes.
  • Decreasing Gap: The pattern is likely based on Subtraction or Division.

Important Notes: "This is the master key for number series," the speaker says. "Don't just randomly guess. Look at how fast the series is growing. If it goes from 5 to 100 in just a few steps, it's not addition, it's multiplication or squares. If it goes from 5 to 20 slowly, it's probably addition. Analyzing the gap is the first step to choosing the right tool."

Question 1: Simple Addition Pattern

Question

3, 6, 9, 12, 15, ?

Answer

18

Explanation

The gap between the numbers is small and constant. Let's find the difference:

  • 6 - 3 = 3
  • 9 - 6 = 3
  • 12 - 9 = 3
  • 15 - 12 = 3

The pattern is a constant addition of 3. Therefore, the next term is 15 + 3 = 18.

Question 2: Increasing Addition Pattern

Question

2, 5, 9, 14, 20, ?

Answer

27

Explanation

The gap is increasing, but not too rapidly. Let's find the difference:

  • 5 - 2 = 3
  • 9 - 5 = 4
  • 14 - 9 = 5
  • 20 - 14 = 6

The difference is increasing by 1 each time (+3, +4, +5, +6...). The next difference to add should be 7. Therefore, the next term is 20 + 7 = 27.

Question 3: Multiplication and Addition Pattern

Question

2, 5, 11, 23, 47, ?

Answer

95

Explanation

The gap is increasing rapidly, suggesting multiplication. The numbers seem to be roughly doubling.

  • 2 * 2 = 4. To get 5, we add 1. (Pattern: ×2 + 1)
  • 5 * 2 = 10. To get 11, we add 1. (Pattern: ×2 + 1)
  • 11 * 2 = 22. To get 23, we add 1. (Pattern: ×2 + 1)
  • 23 * 2 = 46. To get 47, we add 1. (Pattern: ×2 + 1)

The pattern is "multiply by 2, then add 1". Applying this to the last term: 47 * 2 + 1 = 94 + 1 = 95.

Important Notes: "When you see the numbers are almost doubling, always test the 'times two' logic," the speaker advises. "Check `(number * 2) + something` or `(number * 2) - something`. This is a very, very common pattern in competitive exams."

Question 4: Square Pattern (n² - 1)

Question

3, 8, 15, 24, 35, ?

Answer

48

Explanation

The numbers are close to perfect squares. Let's examine their relationship to squares:

  • 3 = 2² - 1
  • 8 = 3² - 1
  • 15 = 4² - 1
  • 24 = 5² - 1
  • 35 = 6² - 1

The pattern is n² - 1, where n starts from 2 and increases. The next term should be 7² - 1 = 49 - 1 = 48.

Important Notes: "This is why you must memorize your squares!" the speaker insists. "If you see 8, 15, 24, 35... these numbers should scream 'squares' at you because they are right next to 9, 16, 25, 36. Recognizing this proximity is the key to solving the problem in seconds."

Question 5: Cube Pattern (n³ + 1)

Question

2, 9, 28, 65, 126, ?

Answer

217

Explanation

The gap is increasing very rapidly, suggesting cubes. The numbers are very close to perfect cubes.

  • 2 = 1³ + 1
  • 9 = 2³ + 1
  • 28 = 3³ + 1
  • 65 = 4³ + 1
  • 126 = 5³ + 1

The pattern is n³ + 1, where n starts from 1. The next term should be 6³ + 1 = 216 + 1 = 217.
