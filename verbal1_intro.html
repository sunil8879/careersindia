Type 1: Questions Based on Language

1. Question

Introducing a man to her husband, a woman named Priya said, “His brother’s father is the only son of my grandfather.” How is Priya related to the man?

2. Answer

Sister

3. Explanation

This is solved by breaking down Priya's statement from her perspective:

  1. "my grandfather": This refers to Priya's own grandfather.
  2. "the only son of my grandfather": The only son of Priya's grandfather must be Priya's father.
  3. "His brother’s father": This is the man's father.
  4. By equating the statements, we understand that the man's father is the same person as Priya's father. If they share the same father, Priya and the man are siblings. Therefore, Priya is his sister.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: For these "pointing" or "introducing" problems, the key is to always start the analysis from the phrase containing "my" or "your." Work backward from that anchor point, resolving each relationship step by step from the speaker's perspective. This method untangles the puzzle logically and avoids confusion.

1. Question

Pointing to a man in a photograph, a boy named Leo said, “His mother’s only daughter is my mother.” How is Leo related to the man in the photograph?

2. Answer

Nephew

3. Explanation

We solve this by deconstructing Leo's statement:

  1. "His mother's only daughter": "His" refers to the man in the photograph. The only daughter of his mother is his sister.
  2. "is my mother": Leo is stating that the man's sister is his (Leo's) mother.
  3. Therefore, Leo is the son of the man's sister. The son of one's sister is their nephew.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: The phrase "only daughter" is a critical piece of information that establishes a definite relationship (sister). Identifying these definitive clues is crucial. The logic flows from the man in the photo to his sister, and then from his sister to her son, Leo.

Type 2: Relation Puzzles

1. Question

There are six members in a family. Alex is the father of David. Ethan is the grandfather of David. Bella is the daughter-in-law of Clara. Frank is the uncle of David. What is the relationship of Clara with Frank?

2. Answer

Data inadequate

3. Explanation

When we attempt to draw a family tree, we get two disconnected groups:

  • Group 1: Ethan is the grandfather of David, and Alex is the father of David. This means Ethan is Alex's father. Frank is David's uncle, so Frank is Alex's brother. This forms one complete family branch: (Ethan → [Alex, Frank] → David).
  • Group 2: Bella is the daughter-in-law of Clara. This means Clara has a son, who is married to Bella. This forms a separate family unit.

Since Clara (from Group 2) and Frank (from Group 1) are in completely separate family trees based on the given information, it is impossible to determine their relationship.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: The goal in puzzle questions is to create a single, unified family tree. If the provided clues result in two or more separate, unlinked family groups, you cannot determine relationships between individuals from different groups. In such cases, the answer is "Data inadequate."

Type 3: Coded Relations

1. Question

Study the following codes:
A $ B means A is the father of B
A @ B means B is the mother of A
A * B means A is the wife of B
A # B means B is the daughter of A
Given the expression `A * B $ C # D @ E`, how is ‘B’ related to ‘D’?

2. Answer

Grandfather

3. Explanation

We build the family tree by decoding the expression step-by-step:

  1. A * B: A is the wife of B. (A is female, B is male).
  2. B $ C: B is the father of C. (C is B's child).
  3. C # D: D is the daughter of C. (C is D's parent, and D is female).
  4. D @ E: E is the mother of D. (This means C and E are a married couple, where C is the father and E is the mother of D).

From the tree, B is the father of C, and C is the father of D. Therefore, B is the paternal grandfather of D.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: In coded relations, it is critical to pay attention to the direction of the relationship. For example, `A @ B` means `B is the mother of A`, not the other way around. Carefully translating each symbol into a generation and gender relationship is the key to building an accurate family tree and avoiding errors.

Chapter 2: Direction Sense Test

Type 1: Based on Distance

1. Question

Nitin starts traveling towards the South and covers a distance of 5 km. He then turns left and travels 8 km. Finally, he takes another left turn and travels 5 km. How far is he from the starting point?

2. Answer

8 km

3. Explanation

Let's trace Nitin's path:

  1. Starts at point A and moves 5 km South to point B.
  2. From South, a left turn is towards the East. He moves 8 km East to point C.
  3. From East, a left turn is towards the North. He moves 5 km North to point D.

His 5 km Southward journey (A to B) is cancelled out by his 5 km Northward journey (C to D). This means his final point D is horizontally aligned with his starting point A. The only distance separating them is the 8 km he traveled East. Therefore, he is 8 km from his starting point.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: The most effective way to solve such problems is to track the net displacement on the North-South and East-West axes. Here, the net North-South movement is `(-5 km) + (+5 km) = 0 km`. The net East-West movement is `+8 km`. The final distance is the result of these net movements.

1. Question

Raju drives 25 km North and turns left, traveling 5 km to reach point ‘O’. He then turns right and covers another 5 km. Afterwards, he turns to the East and drives 5 km. How much distance does he have to travel to go back to the starting point?

2. Answer

30 km

3. Explanation

Let's trace Raju's path from starting point A:

  1. Drives 25 km North from A to a point (let's call it B).
  2. Turns left (West) and travels 5 km to point O.
  3. From O, he turns right (North) and travels 5 km to a point (let's call it C).
  4. From C, he turns East and travels 5 km. Since he just traveled 5 km West, this brings him directly back above point B.

His final position is 5 km North of B. Since B was 25 km North of the start (A), his final position is `25 + 5 = 30 km` North of his starting point. To travel back, he must cover this distance.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: A clear diagram is essential. Notice how the 5 km West and subsequent 5 km East movements cancel each other out horizontally, simplifying the problem to a purely vertical distance calculation from the starting point.

Type 2: Based on Direction

1. Question

I am facing North. I turn 100° in the clockwise direction and then 145° in the anti-clockwise direction. Which direction am I facing now?

2. Answer

North-West

3. Explanation

We can calculate the net rotation:

  1. Clockwise is positive (+100°), and anti-clockwise is negative (-145°).
  2. Net rotation = `+100° - 145° = -45°`.
  3. A negative rotation means an anti-clockwise turn from the starting direction.
  4. Starting from North, a 45° anti-clockwise turn leads to the North-West direction.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: When multiple turns are involved, calculating the net rotation is far more efficient than plotting each turn individually. Treat one direction (e.g., clockwise) as positive and the other as negative, then find the sum to get the final effective turn.

1. Question

A person walks towards his house at 8:00 AM and observes his shadow to his right. In which direction is he walking?

2. Answer

South

3. Explanation

  1. Time: At 8:00 AM, the sun is in the East.
  2. Shadow Direction: A shadow is always cast in the direction opposite to the sun. Therefore, the shadow will be in the West.
  3. Person's Orientation: The person sees the shadow to his **right**. For a person's right-hand side to be pointing West, they must be facing South.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: The logic for shadow problems is fixed and reliable. Step 1: Determine the sun's position (Morning=East, Evening=West). Step 2: Determine the shadow's direction (opposite to the sun). Step 3: Align the person's left or right side with the shadow's direction to deduce their facing direction.

Type 3: Based on Distance and Direction

1. Question

Arun walked 20 m towards South. Then, he turned to his left and walked 25 m. He then turned to his left and walked 20 m. He again turned to his right and walked 15 m. At what distance is he from the starting point and in which direction?

2. Answer

40 m, East

3. Explanation

His total distance from the starting point is the total eastward displacement: `25 m + 15 m = 40 m`. His final direction from the starting point is East.

Logic Technique: Drawing a clear diagram is crucial. The key insight is realizing that the initial 20m South and the subsequent 20m North movements cancel each other out on the vertical axis, simplifying the problem to a straight-line horizontal distance calculation.

Type 1: Letter Coding

1. Question

In a certain code, `GLAMOUR` is written as `IJCNMWP` and `MISRULE` is written as `OGUSSNC`. How will `TOPICAL` be written in that code?

2. Answer

VMRJACJ

3. Explanation

This is a mixed pattern based on letter positions. Analyzing the first word, `GLAMOUR`:

  • G (+2) → I
  • L (-2) → J
  • A (+2) → C
  • M (+1) → N (Middle letter)
  • O (-2) → M
  • U (+2) → W
  • R (-2) → P

The pattern is `+2, -2, +2, +1, -2, +2, -2`. Applying this same complex pattern to the word `TOPICAL` gives the result `VMRJACJ`.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: When a simple `+n` or `-n` shift doesn't work for the whole word, check for an alternating or mixed pattern. Write the positional values of the letters to clearly see the mathematical relationship. Providing two examples (GLAMOUR and MISRULE) confirms that the complex pattern is consistent and must be applied.

Type 2: Number Coding

1. Question

If each letter is assigned an even numerical value starting with A=2, B=4, and so on, what is the total value of the letters for the word ‘LADY’?

2. Answer

84

3. Explanation

The coding rule is `Letter's Value = (Standard Alphabetical Position) x 2`.

  • L: 12th letter → 12 x 2 = 24
  • A: 1st letter → 1 x 2 = 2
  • D: 4th letter → 4 x 2 = 8
  • Y: 25th letter → 25 x 2 = 50

The total value is the sum: `24 + 2 + 8 + 50 = 84`.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: First, clearly define the rule for converting letters to numbers. In this case, it's a simple multiplication of the standard position. Then, apply this rule to each letter of the target word and perform the final calculation (sum, product, etc.) as required.

1. Question

If PALE is coded as 2134 and EARTH is coded as 41590, how is PEARL coded?

2. Answer

24153

3. Explanation

This is a direct substitution code where each letter is assigned a specific digit. We can build a decoding key from the given examples:

  • From PALE → P=2, A=1, L=3, E=4
  • From EARTH → E=4, A=1, R=5, T=9, H=0

Using this key, we can now code the word PEARL:

  • E → 4
  • A → 1
  • R → 5

The resulting code is 24153.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: When you are given full words as examples, the first pattern to check for is direct substitution. Look for common letters between the example words (like 'A' and 'E' here) to confirm that the code for each letter is fixed.

Type 3: Substitutional Coding

1. Question

If ‘white’ is called ‘blue’, ‘blue’ is called ‘red’, ‘red’ is called ‘yellow’, ‘yellow’ is called ‘green’, ‘green’ is called ‘black’, ‘black’ is called ‘violet’, and ‘violet’ is called ‘orange’, what would be the colour of the leaves?

2. Answer

Black

3. Explanation

This is a direct substitution puzzle.

  1. First, identify the actual answer to the question: The real color of leaves is **Green**.
  2. Next, find the code for "Green" in the given substitution list.
  3. The problem states: "‘green’ is called ‘black’".

Therefore, in this coded language, the color of the leaves is Black.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: This type of question has a simple, two-step logic. Step 1: Find the true answer. Step 2: Find the code for that true answer. A common mistake is to follow a chain of substitutions (e.g., leaves are green, green is black, black is violet...). This is incorrect. You only need to apply the substitution once.

Type 4: Chinese Coding

1. Question

In a certain code language:
‘Sue Re Nik’ means ‘She is Brave’
‘Pi Sor Re Nik’ means ‘She is always smiling’
‘Sor Re Zhi’ means ‘Is always cheerful’
What is the code for the word ‘smiling’?

2. Answer

Pi

3. Explanation

We solve this by finding common words and their corresponding common codes across the sentences.

  1. Compare sentence 1 and 2: The common words are 'She is' and the common codes are 'Re Nik'. So, 'She is' = 'Re Nik'.
  2. Compare sentence 2 and 3: The common words are 'is always' and the common codes are 'Sor Re'.
  3. From these, we can deduce: 'is' = 'Re'. This means 'She' = 'Nik' and 'always' = 'Sor'.
  4. Now look at sentence 2 again: ‘Pi Sor Re Nik’ means ‘She is always smiling’. We have already decoded 'Sor' (always), 'Re' (is), and 'Nik' (She).
  5. The only remaining word is 'smiling' and the only remaining code is 'Pi'. Therefore, 'smiling' = 'Pi'.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: The key to Chinese Coding is systematic comparison and elimination. Compare two sentences at a time, identify the common word(s) and the common code(s). This allows you to create a definite match. Repeat this process until you have isolated the code for the word you need.

Type 5: Symbol Coding

In a certain code, `BRAIN` is written as `*%÷#X` and `TIER` is written as `$#+%`. How is `RENT` written in that code?

2. Answer

%+X$

3. Explanation

This is a direct substitution code where each letter corresponds to a specific symbol. We create a key by observing the given examples:

  • From `BRAIN`: B=*, R=%, A=÷, I=#, N=X
  • From `TIER`: T=$, I=#, E=+, R=%

Now we use this key to code the word `RENT`:

Logic Technique: Just like with number substitution, this relies on building a complete decoding key. Finding the common letters ('R' and 'I' in this case) and verifying that their symbols are consistent across both examples confirms that it's a simple one-to-one substitution.

Chapter 4: Matrix Coding

1. Question

A word is represented by only one set of numbers as given in any one of the alternatives. Using the matrices below, identify the set for the word ‘MAPS’.

Options:
a) 65, 22, 43, 02
b) 68, 14, 00, 04
c) 99, 34, 13, 24
d) 95, 01, 31, 40

2. Answer

b) 68, 14, 00, 04

3. Explanation

In matrix coding, a letter's code is formed by its row number first, then its column number. We test the options against the word `MAPS`.

  1. Test Option (b): 68, 14, 00, 04
    • M = 68? Go to Row 6, Column 8 in Matrix-II. The letter is M. (Correct)
    • A = 14? Go to Row 1, Column 4 in Matrix-I. The letter is A. (Correct)
    • P = 00? Go to Row 0, Column 0 in Matrix-I. The letter is P. (Correct)
    • S = 04? Go to Row 0, Column 4 in Matrix-I. The letter is S. (Correct)

Since all the codes in option (b) correctly match the letters of the word MAPS, it is the right answer.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: The most efficient method for solving matrix problems is the **elimination method**. Start with the first letter of the word (M) and the first number in each option. Check option (a): Is M at 65? No, B is at 65. So, option (a) is wrong. Move to option (b): Is M at 68? Yes. Proceed to check the next letter. This process of checking one letter at a time and eliminating wrong options is much faster than finding all possible codes for every letter.

1. Question

How many symbols are there in the above series each of which is immediately preceded by an alphabet and followed by a number?

2. Answer

One

3. Explanation

We need to find the specific pattern: [Alphabet] [Symbol] [Number].

Scanning the sequence, we look for this exact arrangement:

  • ... F 3 * ... (Fails: Symbol is followed by a letter J)
  • ... S 8 5 ... (Fails: No symbol)
  • ... S α↑ ... (Fails: Symbol is followed by a symbol)

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: For condition-based questions, it's crucial to break down the rule into a simple visual pattern (e.g., A#N). Type 2: Finding Relative Positions

1. Question

Which of the following is exactly in the middle of the 10th element from the left and the 8th element from the right end in the given arrangement?

2. Answer

S

3. Explanation

  1. First, identify the two landmark positions:
    • 10th element from the left: Counting from the beginning (`$`), the 10th element is `&`.
    • 8th element from the right: Counting from the end (`3`), the 8th element is `E`.
  2. Next, list the elements located *between* `&` and `E`: `@, P, L, V, S, α, ↑, M, U`.
  3. There are 9 elements in this sub-sequence. The middle element is the 5th one, which is `S`.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: For "middle element" questions, the process is always: (1) Find the first position. (2) Find the second position. (3) Carefully count the items between them to find the exact center. A common mistake is being off-by-one in the count, so be deliberate.

Type 3 & 4: Finding Patterns and Odd One Out

REFERENCE SEQUENCE FOR THIS EXAMPLE:

W I R % 4 J E # 7 M T 2 1 9 B H 3 A $ 9 F Q 5 D G 6 U S P

1. Question

What should come in place of the question mark in the series based on the arrangement?
`R 4 #`   `M 2 B`   `3 $ Q`   `?`

2. Answer

D 6 P

3. Explanation

The logic is that each element in a group is found by moving 7 steps forward from the corresponding element in the previous group.

  • From `R` to `M` is +7 positions. From `M` to `3` is +7 positions. So, the first element of the next group is 7 positions after `3`, which is `D`.

  • From `#` to `B` is +7 positions. From `B` to `Q` is +7 positions. So, the third element is 7 positions after `Q`, which is `P`.

Therefore, the next group is `D 6 P`.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: In series-based questions within a sequence, analyze the relationship between the first elements of each group, then the second elements, and so on. The pattern is usually a consistent "jump" (e.g., always +7) for each corresponding position.

Based on the positions in the arrangement, if `F J @ : G δ α`, then `# 3 S : ?`

Type 1: Linear Arrangement

1. Question

Six people (A, B, C, D, E, F) sit in two rows of three. E is not at any end. D is second to the left of F. C is a neighbor of E and is sitting diagonally opposite to D. B is a neighbor of F. Who will sit opposite B?

2. Answer

E

3. Explanation

This puzzle is solved by combining clues to construct the seating arrangement:

  1. "D is second to the left of F" in a three-person row fixes their positions as `D, _, F`.
  2. "B is the neighbor of F" means B must be in the middle spot, making one row `D, B, F`.
  3. "C is sitting diagonally opposite to D" means if D is at one end of the bottom row, C must be at the opposite end of the top row.
  4. "E is not at any end" and "C is a neighbor of E" places E in the middle of the top row, next to C.
  5. The only remaining person, A, must take the last spot. The final arrangement is:
    Row 1: A E C
    Row 2: D B F

Based on this arrangement, the person sitting opposite B is E.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: The core strategy for seating arrangement puzzles is to **start with 100% sure information**. A clue like "D is second to the left of F" in a three-person row is a definite, fixed piece of information that creates a solid foundation. Build the rest of the arrangement around these fixed clues.

1. Question

Seven people (A, B, C, D, E, F, G) are sitting on a wall facing East. C is on the immediate right of D. B is at an extreme end and has E as his neighbor. G is between E and F. D is sitting third from the South end. Who is sitting to the right of E?

2. Answer

G

3. Explanation

We build the vertical line of people facing East. South is the bottom end.

  1. "D is sitting third from the south end": This is our anchor clue. We place D in the 3rd spot from the bottom: `_, _, D, _, _, _, _`.
  2. "C is on the immediate right of D": Since they face East, "right" is downwards. So, C is in the 2nd spot: `_, _, D, C, _, _, _`.
  3. "B is at an extreme end and has E as his neighbor": B can be at the top or bottom. But the bottom end is already filling up. Let's assume B is at the North (top) end. E must be next to B.
  4. "G is between E and F": This gives us the block `E, G, F`.
  5. Combining these blocks, the top of the line must be `B, E, G, F`. Fitting this into our structure gives the final arrangement from North (top) to South (bottom): `B, E, G, F, D, C, A`.

From the final arrangement, the person sitting to the right (down) of E is G.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: When dealing with directions other than North, be very careful with "left" and "right." If facing East, your right is South (down) and your left is North (up). Always establish a clear frame of reference before placing individuals.

Type 2: Arrangement in a Circle

1. Question

Six people are sitting in a circle. A is facing B. B is to the right of E and left of C. C is to the left of D. F is to the right of A. Now, D exchanges his seat with F, and E with B. Who will be sitting to the left of D after the exchange?

2. Answer

A

3. Explanation

First, we determine the initial arrangement. Assume they are facing the center (Right = Anti-clockwise, Left = Clockwise).

  1. "A is facing B" places them opposite each other.
  2. "B is to the right of E" places E clockwise from B.
  3. "B is to the left of C" places C anti-clockwise from B. This gives the sequence `E, B, C`.
  4. "C is to the left of D" places D anti-clockwise from C.
  5. "F is to the right of A" places F anti-clockwise from A.
  6. The only way to fit this is a clockwise arrangement of: `A, F, C, D, E, B`.
  7. After the exchange: D and F swap. E and B swap. The new clockwise arrangement is `A, D, C, F, B, E`.
  8. The question is: Who is now to the left of D? "Left" is clockwise. The person clockwise from D is A.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: For circular arrangements, remember the key rule: when facing the center, LEFT is CLOCKWISE and RIGHT is ANTI-CLOCKWISE. For problems with an exchange, first solve the initial arrangement completely, then perform the swap and answer based on the new final arrangement.

Type 3: Arrangement in Other Geometrical Shapes

1. Question

Six people (A, M, N, C, O, G) sit around a triangle, three at corners (facing inside) and three in the middle of the sides (facing outside). A sits at a corner. M is an immediate neighbor of A. Two people sit between M and C. O sits to the immediate left of C. N is an immediate neighbor of O. Who sits second to the right of A?

2. Answer

N

3. Explanation

We place people according to the rules, paying attention to their facing direction.

  1. Place A at a corner (facing in). M is a neighbor, so M must be in the middle of a side (facing out).
  2. "Two people sit between M and C." In a circle of six, this means M and C are opposite each other. If M is on a side, C must be at the opposite corner (facing in).
  3. "O sits to the immediate left of C." Since C is at a corner facing inside, his left is clockwise. So O sits on the middle of the side clockwise to C (facing out).
  4. "N is an immediate neighbor of O." The only available spot next to O is the third corner. So N sits at a corner (facing in).
  5. The final person, G, takes the last remaining middle side spot.
  6. The question asks: Who is second to the right of A? A is at a corner facing inside, so his right is anti-clockwise. The first person to his right is M. The second person to his right is N.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: For mixed-direction puzzles (some facing in, some out), the concept of left/right changes for each person. For someone facing IN, right is anti-clockwise. For someone facing OUT, right is clockwise. You must carefully apply the correct rule for each person based on their position.

  2. The expression is now: `4 + 8 - 1 + 3`

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: This is a two-step process that must be done in the correct order. Step 1 is to carefully rewrite the entire expression with the substituted symbols. 1. Question

In a row of 40 boys, Aman is 16th from the left end and Vivek is 18th from the right end. Gagan is 11th from Aman towards the right and 3rd from Vivek towards the right end. How many boys are there in the row?

2. Answer

3. Explanation

This is a trick question where the initial total of 40 is a distractor. We must calculate the total based on the positions.

Rules for Deduction

1. Question

Rule: If one of the statements is negative, the conclusion must be negative.

Statements:
All books are papers.
No paper is small.
Conclusions:
I. No book is small.
II. All books are small.

2. Answer

Conclusion I follows.

3. Explanation

The statement "No paper is small" is negative. Therefore, any valid definite conclusion must also be negative. Conclusion II ("All books are small") is positive, so it is invalid. Conclusion I ("No book is small") is negative and logically follows from the statements. If all books are a part of the "papers" category, and no part of the "papers" category can be "small," then no book can be small.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: This highlights a fundamental rule of syllogistic reasoning. A negative premise (containing 'No' or 'Not') restricts possibilities. A positive premise (containing 'All' or 'Some') expands them. You cannot arrive at a positive, all-encompassing conclusion from a statement that creates a restriction.

1. Question

Rule: If both statements are negative, no conclusion follows.

Statements:
No cat is a dog.
No dog is a rat.
Conclusions:
I. No cat is a rat.
II. Some rats are dogs.

2. Answer

No conclusion follows.

3. Explanation

The statements establish that "Cat" and "Dog" are separate, and "Dog" and "Rat" are separate. However, this provides no information about the relationship between "Cat" and "Rat." They could be separate, or they could overlap. Since we cannot determine their relationship with certainty, no valid conclusion can be drawn.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: Two negative statements create a broken logical chain. The middle term (Dog) fails to connect the other two terms (Cat and Rat). When you see two "No" statements, you can immediately conclude that no definite relationship can be established between the non-common terms.

Solved Examples

1. Question

Statements:
I. Some Apple are Mango.
II. All Mango are Grapes.
III. No Grapes are Guava.
Conclusion:
I. Some Mango being Guava is a possibility.
II. No Apple are Guava.

2. Answer

Neither conclusion I nor II follows.

3. Explanation

  • Conclusion I: From the statements, we know "All Mango are Grapes" and "No Grapes are Guava." This means that absolutely no Mango can be a Guava. Therefore, there is no possibility of "Some Mango being Guava." This conclusion is false.
  • Conclusion II: We know that the part of "Apple" that is "Mango" cannot be "Guava." However, we don't know about the other part of "Apple" (the part that isn't Mango). That part could potentially overlap with "Guava." Since we cannot be 100% certain that *No Apple* is a Guava, this definite conclusion is invalid.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: This question tests the difference between certainty and possibility. A conclusion must be 100% true in all cases to be valid. Conclusion II is invalid because we can imagine a scenario (a Venn diagram) where it is false. Conclusion I (a possibility question) is invalid because the statements make it a 100% certainty that it's false.

1. Question

Statements:
I. Some years are decades.
II. All centuries are decades.
Conclusions:
I. Some centuries are years.
II. Some decades are years.
III. No century is a year.

2. Answer

Either I or III and II follow.

3. Explanation

Let's analyze the Venn Diagram and conclusions:

  • Diagram: A "Decades" circle contains a "Centuries" circle. The "Years" circle overlaps with the "Decades" circle but has no definite relationship with the "Centuries" circle.
  • Conclusion II: "Some decades are years." Since "Some years are decades," the reverse is always true. This conclusion is definitely correct.
  • Conclusions I and III: "Some centuries are years" and "No century is a year." There is no direct link between Years and Centuries. They might overlap, or they might be separate. Therefore, neither I nor III is a definite conclusion. However, they form a **complementary pair** (an "Either/Or" case) because one must be true, they both involve the same terms, and one is positive while the other is negative.

So, the final answer is that Conclusion II is definitely true, and either Conclusion I or Conclusion III must also be true.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: This problem introduces the **"Either/Or"** case, also known as a complementary pair. This special condition applies when: (1) both conclusions are individually uncertain, (2) they feature the same two terms, and (3) one is affirmative (Some) and the other is negative (No). When these conditions are met, the two conclusions form a valid "Either/Or" pair.

Type 1: Counting the Number of Lines

1. Question

How many straight lines are there in the following figure?

2. Answer

3. Explanation

The best way to count lines is to categorize them to avoid double-counting.

  • Horizontal Lines: 3
  • Vertical Lines: 3

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: For counting straight lines, always break the figure down into horizontal, vertical, and diagonal components. Count each category separately and then add them up. This systematic approach ensures you don't miss any lines or count a single line multiple times.

Type 2: Counting the Number of Triangles

1. Question

Trick 1: If a triangle is divided into smaller triangles by lines originating from one vertex to the opposite base, how do you count the total number of triangles?

2. Answer

Number the individual small triangles along the base (1, 2, 3...) and then simply add these numbers together.

3. Explanation

For a triangle divided into 4 parts, the segments are numbered 1, 2, 3, and 4. The total count is the sum of these numbers: `1 + 2 + 3 + 4 = 10` triangles.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: This summation trick is the fastest way to solve this common pattern. It works because it systematically accounts for triangles of all sizes: single-segment triangles, two-segment triangles, and so on, without needing to count them manually.

1. Question

Trick 3: If a square is divided into triangles by diagonals and/or lines passing through the center, what is the shortcut?

2. Answer

Count the number of small, visible triangles and then double that number.

3. Explanation

In a square with two diagonals, there are 4 small triangles. The total number of triangles (including larger ones formed by combinations) is `4 x 2 = 8`. If the square is further divided into 8 small triangles, the total is `8 x 2 = 16`.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: This "count and double" method is a highly effective shortcut specifically for squares and rectangles where all dividing lines intersect at the center. It should not be applied to other shapes or configurations.

1. Question

Trick 4: If a triangle has another inverted triangle within it, touching the midpoints of its sides, what is the shortcut?

2. Answer

Count the number of small, visible triangles and add 1 for the large, outer triangle. An inner triangle creates 4 small ones, so the total is `4 + 1 = 5`.

3. Explanation

An inverted triangle inside a larger one divides it into four small triangles of equal size, plus the large outer triangle itself. If there are two such nested triangles (a triangle within a triangle within a triangle), the formula becomes `4 + 4 + 1 = 9`.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: The logic is that each new, embedded inverted triangle adds exactly 4 new triangles to the count. The final `+1` is for the main, outermost triangle. This is a pattern-recognition shortcut.

Type 3: Counting the Number of Squares

1. Question

Trick 1 (Symmetrical Figure): How do you count the total number of squares in a symmetrical grid (e.g., a 4x4 chessboard)?

2. Answer

Number the small squares along one side (1, 2, 3...) and add the squares of these numbers.

3. Explanation

For a 4x4 grid, the total number of squares is the sum of the squares of the side numbers:

`1² + 2² + 3² + 4² = 1 + 4 + 9 + 16 = 30` squares.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: This formula works because it accounts for squares of all sizes: 4x4 (1²), 3x3 (2²), 2x2 (3²), and 1x1 (4²) squares on a 4x4 board. It's a fundamental shortcut for any n x n grid.

1. Question

Trick 2 (Asymmetrical Figure): How do you count squares in an asymmetrical grid (e.g., a 6x4 grid)?

2. Answer

Multiply the largest row and column numbers, then keep reducing each by one and adding the products until one of them becomes 1.

3. Explanation

For a 6x4 grid:

  • `6 x 4 = 24`
  • `5 x 3 = 15`
  • `4 x 2 = 8`
  • `3 x 1 = 3`

Total number of squares = `24 + 15 + 8 + 3 = 50`.

4. Important Notes

Logic Technique: This method is the standard way to count squares in any m x n rectangle. It systematically counts all squares from the largest possible size (e.g., 4x4 in this case) down to the smallest (1x1).
