Calendar Test

1. Theory: About the Calendar Test

The Calendar Test is a key topic in the Logical Reasoning and Quantitative Aptitude sections of Indian government competitive exams (SSC, Banking, Railways, etc.). It assesses a candidate's ability to understand the structure of a calendar and solve problems related to dates and days of the week.

The core of solving calendar problems lies in the concept of "odd days".

Key Concepts & Techniques

  • Ordinary Year: An ordinary year has 365 days. When we divide 365 by 7, we get a quotient of 52 (weeks) and a remainder of 1. This remainder is the "odd day". So, an ordinary year has 1 odd day.
  • Leap Year: A leap year has 366 days. 366 divided by 7 gives a quotient of 52 (weeks) and a remainder of 2. So, a leap year has 2 odd days.
  • How to Identify a Leap Year:
    1. For a non-century year, it must be divisible by 4 (e.g., 2004, 2016).
    2. For a century year, it must be divisible by 400 (e.g., 1600, 2000 are leap years, but 1700, 1900 are not).
  • Odd Days in Centuries (Diagram): This is a crucial shortcut.
    Number of YearsOdd Days
    100 years5
    200 years3
    300 years1
    400 years0

    (Note: Any multiple of 400 years, like 800, 1200, 1600, 2000, has 0 odd days).

  • Day of the Week based on Odd Days (Diagram): The final number of odd days corresponds to a day of the week.

Types of Questions Asked

  • Finding the Day of a Given Date: The most common type, e.g., finding the day on 15th August 1947.
  • Relative Day Problems: Questions like "If the day before yesterday was Sunday, what will be the day after tomorrow?"
  • Calendar Repetition: Finding when the calendar for a particular year will be the same as a future year.
  • Concept-Based Questions: Questions about the number of leap years, odd days, or occurrences of a specific date.

2. Questions, Answers, and Explanations

  1. Question: What was the day of the week on 15th August 1947?

    A) Thursday
    B) Friday
    C) Saturday
    D) Sunday

    Answer: B) Friday

    Technique: The Odd Day Calculation Method. We calculate the total number of odd days from year 1 up to the given date.

    Explanation:

    The period is 1946 years + the period from 1st Jan 1947 to 15th Aug 1947.

    Step 1: Odd days in 1946 years

    • Break 1946 into centuries and remaining years: 1600 + 300 + 46 years.
    • Odd days in 1600 years = 0 (since it's a multiple of 400).
    • Odd days in 300 years = 1.
    • Odd days in 46 years:
      • Number of leap years = 46 / 4 = 11.
      • Number of ordinary years = 46 - 11 = 35.
      • Total odd days = (11 * 2) + (35 * 1) = 22 + 35 = 57 days.
      • 57 / 7 gives a remainder of 1.
    • Total odd days in 1946 years = 0 + 1 + 1 = 2.

    Step 2: Odd days in the running year (1947)

    (1947 is an ordinary year, so February has 28 days)

    MonthJanFebMarAprMayJunJulAugTotal
    Days3128313031303115227
    Odd Days30323231

    Sum of odd days = 3+0+3+2+3+2+3+1 = 17. (Or 15 for Aug days: `3+0+3+2+3+2+3+15 = 31`).
    31 / 7 gives a remainder of 3.

    Step 3: Total Odd Days

    Total odd days = (Odd days from years) + (Odd days from months) = 2 + 3 = 5.

    According to our Day Code table, 5 corresponds to Friday.

  2. Question: Today is Monday. After 61 days, it will be:

    A) Wednesday
    B) Saturday
    C) Tuesday
    D) Thursday

    Answer: B) Saturday

    Technique: Find the number of odd days in the given period and add them to the current day.

    Explanation:

    1. Each week has 7 days. The day of the week repeats every 7 days.
    2. We need to find the number of odd days in 61 days.
    3. Divide 61 by 7: 61 / 7 = 8 with a remainder of 5.
    4. So, there are 5 odd days.
    5. The required day will be 5 days after Monday.
    6. Monday + 5 days = Saturday.

  3. Question: The calendar for the year 2007 will be the same for which of the following years?

    A) 2014
    B) 2016
    C) 2017
    D) 2018

    Answer: D) 2018

    Technique: A calendar repeats when the total number of odd days becomes a multiple of 7. Count the odd days year by year starting from the given year.

    Explanation:

    For a calendar to repeat, the total number of odd days from the start year must be 0 (or a multiple of 7). Also, the start and end years must be of the same type (both ordinary or both leap years).

    YearOdd DaysTotal Odd Days
    2007 (Ord)11
    2008 (Leap)21 + 2 = 3
    2009 (Ord)13 + 1 = 4
    2010 (Ord)14 + 1 = 5
    2011 (Ord)15 + 1 = 6
    2012 (Leap)26 + 2 = 8 (or 1)
    2013 (Ord)11 + 1 = 2
    2014 (Ord)12 + 1 = 3
    2015 (Ord)13 + 1 = 4
    2016 (Leap)24 + 2 = 6
    2017 (Ord)16 + 1 = 7 (or 0)

    The total number of odd days becomes 7 (which is equivalent to 0) at the end of 2017. This means the calendar for the next year, 2018, will be the same as 2007. (Both 2007 and 2018 are ordinary years).

    Explanation:

    Answer: B) 97

    Technique: Apply the leap year rules for both non-century and century years.

    Explanation:

    1. First, find the number of leap years by dividing by 4: 400 / 4 = 100.
    2. However, this count includes century years that might not be leap years. The century years in this period are 100, 200, 300, and 400.
    3. According to the rule, a century year is a leap year only if it is divisible by 400.
    4. Out of 100, 200, 300, and 400:
      • 100 is not divisible by 400 (Not a leap year).
      • 200 is not divisible by 400 (Not a leap year).
      • 300 is not divisible by 400 (Not a leap year).
      • 400 is divisible by 400 (Is a leap year).
    5. So, we must subtract the 3 century years that are not leap years from our initial count of 100.
    6. Number of leap years = 100 - 3 = 97.

  9. Question 9: The last day of a century cannot be:

    A) Monday
    B) Wednesday
    C) Tuesday
    D) Friday

    Answer: C) Tuesday

    Explanation

    The number of odd days in centuries follows a pattern:
    100 years: 5 odd days (ends on Friday)
    200 years: 3 odd days (ends on Wednesday)
    300 years: 1 odd day (ends on Monday)
    400 years: 0 odd days (ends on Sunday)
    This cycle (5,3,1,0) repeats. So, a century can only end on a Friday, Wednesday, Monday, or Sunday. It can never end on a Tuesday, Thursday, or Saturday.
